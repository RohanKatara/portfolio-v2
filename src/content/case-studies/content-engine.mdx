## Overview

Content Engine takes one long form piece : a blog post, a video transcript, a launch announcement and spits out platform specific variants for every social channel a marketing team cares about. Twitter threads, LinkedIn carousels, Instagram captions, newsletter blurbs, all in the brand's voice.

The pitch to a marketer is simple: write once, ship twelve places. The pitch to me as an engineer was: how tight can the prompt loop get before brand voice starts drifting?

## How it works

The system pipes the source content through a chain of platform specific prompts. Each prompt knows the format constraints of its channel (character limits, optimal structure, hashtag conventions) **and** the brand voice rules. The voice rules are extracted up front from a sample corpus the user provides, everything that follows compares against that corpus instead of "generic helpful AI."

The output preview lets the user accept, edit, or regenerate per channel before anything ships. Nothing posts automatically, the model is the writer, not the publisher.

## What I learned

- Brand voice is the killer feature. Generic LLM output is everywhere; output that *sounds like the user* is rare and hard.
- Format follows function. A LinkedIn carousel and a Twitter thread aren't just length different. They're argument shape different. The prompts have to know that, not just "be shorter."
- Manual review at the end is non negotiable. Marketers will not let an LLM publish for them, and they're right not to.

## Reflection

The interesting tension here was between *automation* (the promise) and *control* (the reality). The version of this product that fully autoposts is a worse product than the one that drafts and asks. I think every AI workflow tool eventually learns that lesson.
